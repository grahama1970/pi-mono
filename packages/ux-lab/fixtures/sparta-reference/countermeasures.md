For the best segment for countermeasure deployment, this is meant to articulate the ideal place to deploy the countermeasure leveraging the following choices: space segment, the development environment, or the ground segment. The space segment is considered to be the spacecraft or spacecrafts if within a constellation. The development segment captures the factories, hardware foundries, the software development organization as well as the Assembly, Test and Launch Operations (ATLO) facilities. The ground segment is meant to capture the operational and maintenance areas for the ground system. This includes the mission operations environments, the antenna environments, the back haul networks, as well as any management network segments for vendors or commercial entities.

Countermeasures represent security concepts and classes of technologies that can be used to prevent a In response to community feedback and growing demand, the SPARTA team conducted a countermeasure prioritization effort to help space stakeholders make informed decisions. This process ranks countermeasures based on their effectiveness, feasibility, and implementation cost, resulting in a tiered structure that highlights which security measures should be prioritized first.

The prioritization uses a structured scoring method that evaluates the CMs efficacy, feasibility, and cost. Efficacy is informed by the average Notional Risk Score (NRS) of the techniques a countermeasure mitigates, giving greater weight to controls that address higher-risk adversary behaviors. Feasibility considers practical implementation constraints such as spacecraft SWaP limitations, architectural compatibility, and Technology Readiness Level (TRL), while cost reflects integration complexity and lifecycle impact on a standardized scale. The final prioritization score is calculated as (Feasibility × Cost) divided by Efficacy, with lower scores indicating higher-priority countermeasures that provide strong mission impact while remaining practical to implement. See SPARTA User Guide for more information as well as this presentation.

Each SPARTA CM is now organized into one of three tiers to reflect its relative criticality, impact, and implementation burden. Tier 1 includes foundational protections that are both high impact and broadly feasible, making them recommended considerations for most missions, while Tier 2 encompasses valuable controls that may involve moderate complexity, cost, or mission-specific tradeoffs. Tier 3 captures advanced, emerging, or niche capabilities that are often higher cost or lower maturity and are best suited for high-risk or highly capable missions. In addition to tiering, countermeasures are categorized by their applicability to the onboard spacecraft, ground and operational environments, development processes, or combinations thereof.
